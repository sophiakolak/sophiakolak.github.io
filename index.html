Research

I am interested in a wide variety of computer science topics from machine learning, robotics, and artificial intelligence to software engineering and automatic program repair. I am also broadly interested in neuroscience and the philosophy of science.

Previously, I worked in the Axel Lab Axel Lab under PI and Nobel Laureate Richard Axel. The lab's goal is to understand how the brain is able to construct an internal representation of reality. My project was to investigate how the olfactory circuit in particular impacts our mental represntation of distance, time, and space. Most of my work involved using recurrent neural networks and deep learning algorithms to find trends in high dimensionality neuron spiking data.

I also participated in the Software Engineering REU program at Carnegie Mellon University this summer. I was advised by Claire Le Goues and Michael Hilton, and worked alongisde Chris Timperley and Afsoon Afzal, studying the Robot Operating System (ROS). Our original idea was to understand the state of testing in robotics code, since very little research had been done on the subject. As I collected more an more data on ROS, however, it became clear that just the software ecosystem alone was incredibly interesting and deserved its own research paper. We just completed and submitted a paper that details all of our findings. I also gave a talk on this research at ROScon in October. Video and slides can be found here!

During the Fall Semester, I conducted research for academic credit with professor Hod Lipson at the Creative Machines Lab. The ambitious and exciting goal of the project was to create a robotic face that could copy human facial expressions by learning from the faces of others. Two MechE students worked on hardware while I worked on software. As this was a massive undertaking with many components, I spent most of the semester building a simulation of the face in C++ using the Titan graphics library. A breif report I wrote on the original simulation's implementation can be found here.

Another ongoing project is my work with Columbia Space Iniative. I am the mission co-lead for Columbia's CS team, recently accepted to compete in the NASA SUITS challenge . For this mission, our team is designing an augmented reality user interface for the Microsoft HoloLens, a proxy for the AR-enabled glass that will be present in next generation space suit helmets. The UI is meant to help astronauts complete tasks with less intervention from Mission Control, in preparation for deep space exploration. Our faculty advisor and mentor is professor Steven Feiner, PI of the Columbia Computer Graphics and User Interfaces Lab. Here Here is a link to our accepted proposal an initial design plan.
