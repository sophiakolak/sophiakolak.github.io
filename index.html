Research

I am interested in a wide variety of computer science topics, from robotics, machine learning, and artificial intelligence to software engineering and automatic program repair.

Previously, I worked in the Axel Lab Axel Lab under PI and Nobel Laureate Richard Axel. The lab's goal is to understand how the brain is able to construct an internal representation of reality. My project was to investigate how the olfactory circuit in particular impacts our mental represntation of distance, time, and space. Most of my work involved using recurrent neural networks and deep learning algorithms to find trends in high dimensionality neuron spiking data.

I also participated in the Software Engineering REU program at Carnegie Mellon University this summer. I was advised by Claire Le Goues and Michael Hilton, and worked alongisde Chris Timpereley and Afsoon Alleb, studying the Robot Operating System (ROS). Our original idea was to understand the state of testing in robotics code, since very little research had been done on the subject. As I collected more an more data on ROS, however, it became clear that just the software ecosystem alone was incredibly interesting and deserved its own research paper. We are currently still working on a paper that details all of our findings, and I will be speaking about this research at ROScon in October.

undergraduate reading group

Programming languages papers are notoriously hard to read. But that doesn't stop us from trying! Often it can be even more overwhelming when around PhD students and professors who have been studying these topics for years. I started this group because I wanted to create an environment where undergraduates interested in programming languages could learn and ask questions without pressure or fear of judgment. We meet weekly (over pizza of course) and have a fun time figuring out these tough papers together. If you're at IU, email me to join our email list.
